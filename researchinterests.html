Research Interests

Native Pollinator Conservation

Insect pollinators are valued for their ecosystem services including cross pollinating over 87% of flowering plants which increases genetic diversity and crop yield. These ecosystem services are at risk given the worldwide decline pollinator communities are experiencing. Factors including changes in land use, agricultural intensification, habitat fragmentation, alien speices, pathogens, and the interaction among these factors are driving these declines. I am interested in developing strategies to help combat these declines by conserving these communities of pollinators and protecting the ecosystem services they provide.

Human Behavioral Psychology and Pollinator Conservation

Currently, I am investigating the drivers and limitations related to the socio-psychological determinants of human pro-environmental behaviors. Specifically, we are conducting quantitative surveys from various populations of persons that will measure what determines the likelihood of them exhibiting beavhiors that support pollinator conservation. This work is a crucial step forward in supporting pollinator conservation. In light of the growing attention and concern for pollinators, scientists and governmental agencies have developed best management practices (BMPs) aimed at supporting pollinator communities. Unfortunately, there remians a gap between the biophysical research guiding these policies and hte human socio-ecological factors that affect the the successful adoption of such policies.

Recent literature discussing other pro-environmental behaviors (e.g., sustainability, recycling, etc.) have identified socio-psychological determinants that or limit adoption of new behaviors by individuals. Determinants of human behavior such as norms, attitudes, and beliefts can be incorporated into policies and educaitonal/outreach programs that will support conventional programming focusing on education, incentives, and regulation. My goal is to first assess these socio- psychological determinants of people from diverse populations to determine the best path forward to developing more effective educational and outreach activites seeking to increase adoption of pollinator conservation BMPs.

Protecting Pollinators in Urban Landscapes Conference, Athens GA

The University of Georgia hosted the return of the Protecting Pollinators in Urban Landscapes Conference October 10-12, 2022. There was great interest in involving individuals in urban environments in pollinator conservation. I was grateful for the opportunity to share some preliminary results from a survey of over 1,500 Georgians related to their intent to adopt polliantor conservation best management practices (BMPs). These results are being processed and written into a manuscript and driving revisions to future versions of a measurement tool to better understand the drivers of intention to adopt pollinator conservation BMPs. Effects of Land Management Decisions on Native Pollinator Communities

Prescribed Fire Effects on Pollinators

Research I completed during my PhD. focused on the effects of the spatial characteristics of fire as an ecological disturbance on native pollinator communities in the Southeastern United States pine forests. Prescribed fires are a common land management tool used to approximate the historic low-severity fires that were common throughout the Southeastern U.S. pine forests. While the communities have experienced the disturbances caused by the historic fire regimes, the recent management of prescribed fires has limited the range of fire regime conditions present in these habitats. Previous research has investigated the impact the frequency, seasonality, and severity of fire has on biodiversity. This work sought to advance our understanding of how the spatial extent of fire and landscape heterogeneity may impact biodiversity, specifically the pollinator communities present in these forest habitats.

I sampled pollinator communities in both Loblolly and Longleaf pine forests where burn unit sizes range from 200 - >1000 ha. We measured the recolonization of burn units by pollinators in the years following a fire. We compared pollinator communities sampled from the edges and interiors of large burn units to determine if the spatial characteristics of the prescribed burns impacted the pollinator communitiy. We tested the "pyrodiversity hypothesis" (Martin and Sapsis 1992), which argues that increasing the heterogeneity of fire characteristics in a landscape (pyrodiversity) will result in an increase in the biodiversity present within the same habitat.

IPM Strategies that Support Beneficial Insects

My thesis focused on how conservation biological control and cultural control Integrated Pest Management (IPM) methods like inter-croping floral resources and varied planting dates helps to curtail the effects of pest insects. Conservation biological control includes methods that support an exisiting natural enemy and other beneficial insect populations that can suppress insect pest populations and protect yield. The practice of inter-croping floral resources can provide important access to a variety of nectar, pollen, and other resources that can be lacking in monoculture farming habitat. Farmers can apply multiple IPM methods to employ stronger control over pest insects. By adjusting the date crops are planted the timing of peak pest infestations will be desynced and allow plants to reach a level of maturity where their natural defenses can fend off pest insects.

I sampled beneficial insects among small-scale organic squash fields that emplyed inter-croping floral resources and varied planting dates. Results from the two-year study indicated inconsistent results where a reduction in the squash bug abundance occured with an increase in natural enemies at one of the year-site combinations, but more commonly we observed an increase in natural enemies and beneficial insects but not a reduction in squash bug abundance nor yield loss. These IPM tools require further study to understand how to best utilize the naturally occuring predator/prey dynamics to support crop production.
